Hi, I'm Diego.

I’m an MD/PhD candidate at the Yale School of Medicine, fully funded by the Medical Scientist Training Program.

I think a lot about virtual tissues, control of cell systems, AI, complexity theory, regenerative biology, BCIs, and a world where death isn’t mandatory.

This year, alongside my medical studies and rotations in PhD labs, I’m part of the 2025 Nucleate Activator Program, working on next generation chips for human computer interfaces.

If there weren’t still a million ways to improve human health using science and technology, you might find me highlining, painting, writing, or talking about the philosophy I coined called Optimistic Absurdism.

Please accept this website and my writings as an untethered handshake into the electronic world and consider reaching out if you find we’re on the same wavelength.

Tracing back some of the formative experiences that got me here and turned me into who I am today:

Before Yale, I worked for 2 years at the Harvard Stem Cell Institute on lineage tracing, single cell technologies, and multi-omic profiling tools in the hematology oncology lab of Dr. Fernando Camargo.

For undergrad, I was fortunate to get admitted into the Columbia Honors program and Brown University, though I decided to accept a full ride offer to study Integrative Neuroscience as a National Hispanic scholar at Fordham University. My studies involved computational, cellular, molecular, and behavioral neuroscience, with minors in Biochemistry & Philosophy. I also competed as a Division I athlete on Fordham's Swim and Dive team (Springboard Diving, 1m, 3m).

In NYC, I investigated epigenetic sex differences that occur in the brain reward area as a result of early-life stress in Dr. Marija Kundakovic’s lab and published on tissue engineering/drug design using molecular dynamics simulations with Dr. Ipsita Banerjee.

During COVID, I also interned at the Brain Injury Research Center at Mt. Sinai and volunteered at the “Stay in Step" Spinal Cord Injury Rehabilitation Center in my hometown of Tampa, Florida.

Between science and athletics, I took private seminars in the Philosophy of Perception, wrote essays on existential AI risk and neuroethics, and took a graduate course in the Philosophy of Science. For the well initiated, I might be best described as adjacent to ethical emotivism and Pyrrhonian skepticism, yet still a livewired and hopefully agentic optimist. I think the best way towards a wonderful future is by facing our hardest challenges head on.
